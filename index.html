BIDS | fMRIPrep

BIDS stands for the Brain Imaging Data Structure, a standarized format for the organization and description of neuroimaging and behavioral data. By using a standardized naming convention, data can be easily shared and understood by other researchers.

fMRIPrep is a functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI) data preprocessing pipeline that is designed to provide an easily accessible, interface that is robust to variations in scan acquisition protocols. It performs basic processing steps (coregistration, normalization, unwarping, noise component extraction, segmentation, skull-stripping, etc.) providing outputs that can be easily submitted to a variety of group level analyses, including task-based or resting-state fMRI, graph theory measures, and surface or volume-based statistics. The fMRIPrep pipeline uses a combination of tools from well-known software packages, including FSL, ANTs, FreeSurfer and AFNI. This pipeline was designed to provide the best software implementation for each state of preprocessing.

CONN

A versatile toolbox used within MATLAB and SPM for performing seed-to-voxel and ROI-to-ROI connectivity analyses as part of resting-state and task-based fMRI workflows.

Data Collection | Design

Qualtrics

Qualtrics is a user-friendly, feature-rich survey platform that I’ve used to design data collection tools, event RSVPs, and feedback forms with logic branching and dynamic question display.

REDCap

REDCap is a secure web application for building and managing online surveys and databases. I've used it to store participant data and survey responses. You can use branching logic to display questions based on previous responses, and calculated fields for real-time scoring of behavioral instruments. It is HIPAA compliant as well for storing identifiable information. It also allows us to enable e-consent for virtual participant visits.

Experience


I have been in the field of neuroscience for more than 13 years now. I started as a research assistant under Dr. Soojin Park at Johns Hopkins University investigating scene perception in humans using fMRI in the Visual Cognitive Neuroscience Lab. This was my first experience helping design an experiment, conducting literature searches, and analyzing and collecting data.

My next position was as a Research Study Technician and Lab Manager, helping Dr. Karin Schon get her newly founded Brain Plasticity & Neuroimaging Lab up and running at Boston University. Here we sought to understand how exercise and physical fitness impacted brain health in younger and older adults. I learned to conduct fitness testing to estimate VO2max, a measure of aerobic fitness, became an expert in manual segmentation of hippocampal subfields (brain regions important for learning and memory and impacted by exercise and aging) on structural MRIs, and helped build and maintain data respositories.

I did my doctoral research with Dr. Chantal Stern, a pioneer in the field of fMRI. My dissertation work focused on the neural substrates of egocentric (self-centered) and allocentric (world-centered) spatial navigation. Here, I developed a novel experiment using a Python based software package, led all aspects of the study from design to data collection to model-based analyses and dissemination of results. During this time I hosted a webinar and created training materials on hippocampal subfield tracing, and was also invited as one of 32 experts to a retreat at the Universidad de Castilla-La Mancha dedicated towards harominization of segmentation protocols.

Upon the successful defense of my dissertation, I was offered a position as a Postdoctoral Associate in the Brain Plasticity & Neuroimaging Lab, where I had worked previously as a tech and lab manager. The lab was just beginning two major R01 studies and I was able to give my expertise and provide lab-wide operational support, correct legacy errors from pre-study preparations, and develop data collection and analysis pipelines. Additionally, with no prior experience in event planning or promotion, I organized and co-led a public Brain Health Forum, overseeing logistics, AV setup, catering, and community outreach. I served as a featured speaker and engaged ~35 attendees from 70 RSVPs. Through of all of this we were able to hit multiple project milestones under tight time and resource constraints

Over my last four positions I have had to wear MANY different hats. I have taught myself to program to build experimental tasks and perform model-based analyses, the logistics involved with planning, promotion, and hosting an event, and the importance of teamwork and leadership. All of the obstacles and problems I have faced have made me into a better, stronger scientist and person and I can't wait for my next challenge!
